Cutting Edge technology!

My Sawmill is a cheap chineese import. It was delivered 50cm shorter than my spesifications,

without the extra blades I ordered. Next the holes for the wheel adjustment had been drilled

with the wrong diameter for tapping, so the threads busted out.

Next it was the aluminium housing for the worm-gear that lifts the saw up and down.

Who could have forseen that a 6mm thick mounting with two m8 bolts could experience a problem

when holding up 170kg of pure saw...

Then it was the spring-loaded crank, with the locking pin for indexing plate.

Closest I've been to losing a finger, was when the bolt holding the crank in place

came loose. The crank, was then pushed out by the spring, disengaging the locking pin.

Leaving the saw in freefall, and the crank in swing. Now going backwards through a

worm-gear may divide the torque, but angular momentum is multiplied, and I have

a third bump on my index-finger to prove it.

Further we have that the holes that connect the rails and sleepers are in the

wrong place, so the whole track must skew slightly to fit the spann of the rollers

Oh and what about the blade guides that where mounted too high, so the steel

forks had to be mounted at an angle, guess how I snaped my first blade...

Oh and I have yet to recive an e-mail from any one of the four people I've

contacted at the company.

With all that aside, there are some positives. This thing is solid, spec wise

it's comparable to a Woodland Mills HM126, but with an aditional 250kg of steel.

And when I made new blade-guides for mine, I gained an aditional 12cm cut-width.

Also almost everything is made of mild-steel, so it's easy to weld.

Wich is good, since you will be doing alot of it, to get the saw in running order.

But after that it's a quite capable machine. Would I buy it again? Absolutley not.

A few trips to the scrap-yard, the hardware store, and a weekend of hard work,

you could make something better yourself, at a fifth of the cost.

By JKnudsen
