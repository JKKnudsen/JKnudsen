Spruced up!

On my propertie there is two varieties of Spruce. Mostly Sitka spruce(Picea Sitchensis),

but also the Norwegian Spruce(Pica Abies). Almost everything that goes through my

sawmill is Sitka. Initially from Alaska, It was introduced to Norway in the 1900's.

It grew faster, and was more resiliant to saline conditions than the Norwegian spruce.

It also has a higher elastic modulus(11GPa), vs (9.7Gpa) for Norwegian. Despite both having

similar densities of (425kg/m3) and (405kg/m3) respectivly.

These days the Sitka spruce is concidered an invasive spicies in Norway. Mostly because

it's only 100years since it was introduced. Ulike the "Norwegian" spruce introduced

about 2-3k years ago, And Pine 9k years ago. For the first 2500 years of humans living in

Norway, there where no threes. Other than possebly small refuges in the glaciers.

Where Pine, Birch and Hassle could be found. The early occurance of Hassle, is curious,

and highly indicative of human or animal involment.

Now spruce is great for lumber and outdoor furniture. But where it shines the most is

as a varnished tone-wood. Both Sitka and Norwegian spruce gives a rich tone. But as in most

things, we must comprimise. Where Norwegian spruce, tends to have a warmer tone.

Because of it's lower elestic modulus, It will carry the lower register, but tend to distort

in the high register. For Sitka it's the exact opposite. It has excellent projection,

a clear high register, but it lacks bottom. Now this should be taken with a grain of salt.

There are many factors that affect what frequencies couple with the sound-board.

A Norwegian spruce with dense grains, will be stiffer than a spongy Sitka.

The straightness of grain is also important for predictable results. Curves and waves

in the grain can introduce transverse modes, that gulp up energy that should have

been sound-pressure in the sound-hole.
