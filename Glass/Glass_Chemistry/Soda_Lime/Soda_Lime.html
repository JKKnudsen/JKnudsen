Enjoy a glass of Soda-Lime!

By far the most common glass is Soda-Lime. And for this reason it's also the one I work with the most.

It is however, rather unforgiving, and is prone to thermal shock and cracking.

In general there is two varieties of Soda-Lime, container and flatt.

Container glass is used for jars, vessles, and in general objects that are blown.

Flatt glass is used for windows and casting. Both glasses are quite similar in melting point, as

well as optical and mechanical properties. The main differance is in viscosity vs temperature,

where flatt glass has substancially lower viscosity for the same temperature. An important concideration

when you source your material. Casting a telescope blank? Flatt glass. Blowing a flask? Container glass.

Now some processes, like draping, could benefit from the viscosity of container glass. But making the pane

to be draped, yield better results for flatt. There are intermediate viscosity Soda-Lime, like

the Standard No.710. But most would probably just use flatt, and watch the drape carefully.

Most of my experiance with Soda-Lime is in the form of lampvorking simple vacuum-tubes(triode,nixie).

and casting, slumping and draping. As I lack the equipment for proper glass-blowing. But I'm currently

working on it, but got a bit stuck in the process of Aluminium-Oxide extraction

from wood-ash(see. Orbit-Process). Intended for fire-bricks and abrasives.

The processing of wood-ash also yields other usefull minerals for glass and ceramics. (see Wood-Ash)

For instance, the typical green tint to most glass, is the result of Iron(II)Oxide(ferrous oxide).

Adding more will give the green glass typically used in bottles. The brown bottles is the result

of Iron(III)Oxide(ferric oxide). From other sources than wood-ash, you can use Copper(I)Oxide(cuprus oxide)

to make a nice red, or a salt like Copper(II)Sulfate to make a greenish-blue. This is pretty much

the extent of my experience with colored glass from batch. The oxides and salts nessesary for

the entire glass color gamut are pretty expensive. So whenever you see someone throw away a glass

object with unusuall colors, definitly concider taking it. Cobalt(II)Oxide(blue) is pretty common,

but keep an eye out for Potassium Dichromat(Green/Yellow), Sodium Diuranate(fluorescent green),

Gold(III)Chloride(ruby red).
